Chapter 2 Equity and Redistribution of Income and Wealth

Equity, Equality and Efficiency

Equity occurs if a society distributes its resources fairly among its people. Equity has two aspects:

  • horizontal equity where consumers and others with the same circumstances should pay the same level of taxation.
  • vertical equity where taxes should be fairly apportioned between the rich and poor in a society.

Equality is not the same as equity. Equality is about treating everyone in the same way. It aims to promote fairness, but it can only work if everyone starts from the same position and needs the same help.

Absolute and Relative Poverty

Poverty exists when familites do not have enough money or access to resources to be able to experience a reasonable standard or living. This includes having access to food, housing, decent education, healthcare, water supply and sanitation.

Although global poverty is falling, it is still that case that people born in poverty are much more likely to remain poor. A few may escape the "cycle of poverty" but these are the exception.

Extreme poverty is defined by the World Bank as 'the international poverty line of living on less than $1.90 per day.'

Absolute poverty is defined as being when household income is below a certain level that makes it impossible for a person to meet the basic needs of life. In this state of poverty, those affected are not able to benefit from economic growth and rising living standards. They are trapped in a cycle of poverty.

Relative povery compares the income of a household with the average income for their country. Typically, where this is 50% or less than the average, the household is said to be in relative poverty. Households have money but only just enough to meet basic needs. Economics growth is an important factor than can determine whether a household moves out of relative poverty.

Policies Towards Equity and Equality

One way to redistribute income is to pay benefits out of government spending to those on low incomes. This money is raised through taxes and is paid to low income persons and families in order to increase their disposible income.

There are three types of such benefits:

  • Means-tested benfits - These benefits are only paid to those on low incomes. They are targeted directly at those who are seen to be most in need. An example would be unemployment benefits. But this could create a disincentive to work known as the poverty trap. It is where a person or family is financially worse off when one or more members are working rather than living off the range of benefits availible to them.
  • Universal benefits - These are paid out to everyone in certain categories, regardless of their income or wealth. Examples include universal state pensions and child benefit. This can overcome the two problems associated with mean-tested benefits. However, they imply paying out money to many who do not need it and therefore tend to be expensive to operate.
  • Universal Basic Income - This is an unconditional payment made at regular intervals by the government, regardless of earnings or employment status. The principal advantage of this approach is that it reduces poverty while encouraging those receiving the benefit to enter or stay in employment. However. universal payments may be given to those who do not need them, depriving others who might need extra assistance.

Negative income tax is a way of dealing with the weaknesses of the previous benefits regimes. Under negative income tax, people earning below a certain income threshold receive supplemental cash payments from the government, rather than paying taxes.

Key Terms

Equality: where everyone is treated in the same way.

Extreme Poverty: where a household exists on less than $1.90 a day.

Relative Poverty: where household income in 50% or less than the average income.

Means-tested benefits: benefits that are paid only to those whose incomes fall below a certain level.

Poverty-trap: where an individual or a family are better off on means-tested benefits rahter than working.

Universal Benefits: benefits availible to all irrespective of income or wealth.

Universal Basic Income: a regular unconditional cash payment made by the government.

Negative Income Tax: money paid out by the government to those earning below an agreed annual fixed benefit limit.
