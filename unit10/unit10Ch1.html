Unit 10: Government Macroeconomic Intervention

Chapter 1 Government Macroeconomic Policy Objectives

Inflation

Governments aim for low and stable inflation. Governments typically set their central banks a target inflation rate to achieve. Their chances of achieving their target are influenced by a range of factors.

Other countries' inflation rates can influence another country's inflation rate. For instance, a country's firms may import raw materials or capital goods from a country that is experiencing high inflation. If it cannot source these from elsewhere, the country's costs of production will rise and likely push up the country's inflation rate.

A government and a central bank's ability to implement anti-inflation policy tools is also influenced by what other governments and central banks are doing. If a government raises its income tax and other countries do not, the country may lose some of its skilled workers and multinational companies as they choose to move to a different country.

Balance of Payments Stability

Governments usually aim for the total credit items and debit items in the current account of the balance of payments to be equal in the long run. A current account deficit reduces aggregate demand and can result in external debt. A current account surplus can be inflationary and can involve an opportunity cost of forgone imports.

Governments also seek to avoid large changes in the current account balance. Fluctuations in the current account balance can result in changes in the exchange rate. Changes in the exchange rate can create uncertainty, which may reduce investment and economic growth.

Unemployment

Governments seek to keep unemployment low and of short duration.

The quicker the unemployed can return to work, the less output will be lost and the lower government spending on unemployment benefits will be. If workers only experience only short periods of unemployment, they may not lose much income. They are also unlikely to miss out on much training or technological developments.

Economic Growth

Governments may aim for economic growth or increase in the rate of economic growth if the economy has been experiencing a negative output gap.

Expansionary fiscal/monetary policy

To achieve this, a government may use expansionary fiscal or monetary policies. If national output does rise, unemployment is likely to fall. However there is a risk that demand-pull inflation may occur if aggregate demand is too much. The effect on the current account of an increase in national output is uncertain. There may be a rise in exports, but more raw materials and capital goods may be purchased to assist in the production of more goods/services. Some of the extra income may be spent on imported goods/services as well.

Higher output may not be sustainable if it causes depletion of non-renewable resources and damages the environment.

Supply-side policy

A government may also promote potential economic growth by using supply-side policy. Expanding productive capacity should reduce the chances of an economy experiencing demand-pull inflation. By keeping costs of production down, an increase in productive capacity may increase international competitiveness and so improve the balance of payments.

The impact on unemployment is more uncertain. Improvements in education and training will increase the skills of workers, which should make them for attractive to employers. However, if demand for the goods/services is lacking, these workers will not be employed. Support for technological improvements is likely to create some new jobs but also end the need for other jobs.

Economic Development

Development is to improve the quality of life. A better quality of life generally calls for higher incomes but it also involves better education, higher standards of health and nutrition, less poverty, a cleaner environment, more equality of opportunity, greater individual freedom and a rich cultural life.

Sustainability

Very rapid economic growth may be achieved, but this may be at the expense of living standards and quality of life of future generations if it results from the reckless use of resources.

Sustainable development occurs when output increases in a way that does not harm the needs of future generation.

Redistribution of Income and Wealth

Governments may take some money from the rich and use it to help those on low incomes either in the form of cash benefits or in the form of the provision of goods and services such as subsidised housing or free healthcare.

The extent to which governments redistribute income varies. This is because they have different views on how significant can be the disincentive effect to work and enterprise of progressive taxation.

Key Terms

Sustainable Development: development that ensures that the needs of the present generation can be met without harming the well-being of future generations.
