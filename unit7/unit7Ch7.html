Chapter 7 Growth and Survival of Firms

Internal and External Growth of Firms

Firm can grow in two main ways: Internal Growth and External Growth.

Internal growth is when a firm decides to retain some of the profit and put it back into the busines in the form of investments to increase the productive capacity. Most investments will occur when the national economy is approaching a boom period.

External growth is when the business expands by joining with others via takeovers or mergers.

Both types of growth can be taking place at the same time. External growth may be a quicker and cheaper route for firms than Internal growth, especially when there are high fixed costs.

Firms can also grow through diversification, where a firm produces/sells a range of different products. This spreads the business risk. If one branch of its activity is stagnating, there will be revenue from other branches to keep the firm afloat. Sometimes large firms can use the same production facilities to produce various products, allowing them to benefit from economies of scope.

Integration

Integration refers to the ways in which the individual parts of a firm have come together, which could be through:

  • a merger whereby two firms agree to join up with each other.
  • an aquisition or purchase whereby one firm takes over control of another.

Horizontal integration involves the merger or aquisition of a business that is in the same sector of the industry. The prime motive for this growth is the reap the benefits of economies of scale. Research and development costs can be pooled, production plants can be rationalised, marketing costs reduced and so on. Horizontal integration can also lead to access to competition and increased market power. By reducing competition there is opportunity to make abnormal profits. For example, Air France merging with Dutch airline KLM.

Vertical integration is where a firm grows by moving into a forward or backward stage of production process. For example, a manufacturer moving into retail (forward) or taking control over its raw materials (backward). Vertical integration can improve the security and quality of the supplies as well as reduce supply chain costs. There can be disadvantages, such as higher costs if the newly aquired businesses are not effectively intergrated. For instance, Nestle is involved in the production of coffee beans, cocoa and milk as well as in their processing and distribution to retailers and consumers.

Cartels

A cartel is a formal agreement between member firms in an industry to limit competition. The agreement may involve fixing the quantity produced by each cartel member or by fixing the price of the product. A cartel maximises profits in the same way as a monopolist.

OPEC (Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries) is the best known, long-standing example of a cartel. It currently ahs 13 members and membership is open to any countries that are substantial net exporters of crude petroleum. The oil industry will continue to be dominated by a few big players, mainly because of the high fixed costs and risks associated with exploration and drilling.

The long-term survival of a cartel depends on the high barriers to entry. However there are threats to a cartel such as:

  • the possibilty of a price war, where one firm breaks rank to capture a bigger market share.
  • some members may have higher costs than others, resulting in fewer profits due to agreed fixed price.
  • if there is no dominant member that has power to control others.
  • legal obstacles such as in the EU and USA where cartels are illegal since they restrict competition and do not act in the best interest of consumers.

Tip: when writing about a cartel, remember that OPEC is the only remaining example of a cartel and that its power has been severely reduced in recent years. Other cartels which were set up in agricultural markets have collapsed.

The Principal-agent Problem

The principal-agent problem occurs when one person (the agent) makes decisions on behalf of another person (the principal).

In making decisions about the growth of firms, large firms, especially with shareholders, may experience the principal-agent problem.

The problem is that the principal does not know how the agent will act; the principal is also not sure that the agent will act in the principal's best interests. If the agent successfully acts in their own best interet, they can enhance their own career development. However, the principal is not fully aware of what the growth plan involves. This is called the agency cost.

The principal-agent problem is an example of market failure due to information failure. The principals should be the ones in the best position to determine a firm's future growth, but this is often not the case and leads to a misallocation of resources.

Key Terms

Conglomerate: a company with a large number of diversified businesses.

Economies of scope: where a reduction in average total cost is made possible by a firm changing the different goods it produces.

Diversification: where a firm grows through the production or sale of a wide range of different products.

Horizontal integration: where a firm merges or acquires another in the same line of business.

Vertical intergration: where a firm grows by producing backwards or forwards in its supply chain.

Principal-agent problem: where one person (the agent) makes decisions on behalf of another person (the principal).
